Syllabus

Welcome to the course Intelligent Systems. The objective of the course is to make you acquainted with methods and technologies for building Intelligent Systems, in other words, the technological side of Artificial Intelligence.

In the follow-up of this course, the Project IS in period 3, you will have to build and evaluate the performance of your own intelligent agent, one that can perform "rationally" in a computerized game (the Austrian card game "Schnapsen"). The intelligence of the agents will come from (some of) three concepts, problem-solving through a systematic exploration of state-spaces of all options of possible actions and behavior, representation of the knowledge of a problem, and automated learning, e.g. of winning strategies.

In this course, we introduce the basic formalisms that are necessary to achieve rationality: uninformed and informed search (concepts such as A*), adversarial search (MinMax with alpha/beta pruning), logical agents and automated reasoning, representation of uncertain and vague information as well as learning (simple Naive Bayes as well as the basic concepts of Neural Networks and Deep Learning).

Grading of Assignments

Make sure to fill in your thought process and explanations for every task, even if you did not manage to solve it correctly. It is better to submit a partially incorrect assignment on time than to submit a completely correct assignment late. 

If a task asks you to report or explain please make sure to include all relevant details.  As a rule of thumb try to write at least more than one sentence for these types of tasks. 

Steps for getting help for assignments

  • First: search in the Slack if the problem has been previously solved
  • Second: Google the error 
  • Third: Book a practical slot with a TA in this sheet

Only if you have tried all these options and you still haven't solved your issue, start a thread in the #assignment_questions channel. Be sure to mention all the relevant information - your OS, version of Python, which IDE you are using, what you've tried before, and the full error. If you see an error in the Slack channel that you know how to solve please help out your classmates.

Messaging TAs directly is a last resort. Only do this if you've exhausted all other options or your issue is personal in nature and you do not wish to discuss it publicly. Prioritize email over slack DMs. Keep in mind that most probably you are not getting any response past 18:00 or on weekends.

Assessment / Grading

Your final grade for this course will be based on two factors: 

  1. Individual Exams
    1. Partial exam A 22 November 35% 
    2. Partial exam B 20 December 35%
  2. Practical 30% (11 parts in week 2-7, 5% each, pass/fail)

To pass the course, you will need to pass both of the two parts (Exams and Practical).

You will need to pass at least 7 of the 11 practical assignments. The grade for the practical will be the number of passed assignments. The grade for the practical is determined as follows:

  • 7 passed gets 6.5 grade
  • 8 passed gets 7.5 grade
  • 9 passed gets 8 grade
  • 10 passed gets 9 grade
  • 11 passed gets 10 grade

For each of the partial exams you need at least a grade of 5, and when averaged the grade for the two exams has to be a pass-grade (>= 5.5). Failing to satisfy those rules will mean that you fail the course.

If you passed the course, and participated in at least 6 out of 7 Working Groups, an additional 0.3 points will be added to the weighted average of exam results and practical result.

The final result is then rounded: with the expection of between 5.0 and 6.0, we round to the nearest .5, so 7.2 points will be grade 7, 8.3 will be grade 8.5, 6.7 a grade 6.5 and 9.8 a grade 10. Between 50 and 60 points we will be round to the clostest natural number. So 5.4 is grade a 5 (and thus a fail), an 5.5 will be grade 6 (and thus a pass). This is in line with the TER of the study program.

 Partial results are valid for 1 year.

Preparation

You might ask whether there is anything you can do to prepare for the course in the next few days. The answer is yes, there is one thing I really recommend you to do:

Learn Schnapsen (see next bullet)

If otherwise, you wanna get ahead of things:

  • read the reader with some interesting material.
  • watch video lectures. There is a lot of nice stuff out there introducing Artificial Intelligence. Watch it. But there is plenty more available, e.g. videos over alphaGO, etc. Nock yourself out.
  • There are also videos from a previous (more advanced) version of the course. The content is a bit denser, and parts will be different, but watching the lectures can be good preparation. 

Schnapsen

The only thing that would really be beneficial as a preparation for the course is to learn Schnapsen. Schnapsen is the Austrian version of the game 66, and if things go well you will write an intelligent agent that plays Schnapsen. Schnapsen is a two-player card game, and we will play the variant for 2 players.

Here is a good explanation of the rules, and this website gives you some hints on what a good strategy can be (which is important as we want to formalize these kinds of strategies in order to make our agents more intelligent).

So, get a standard deck of cards, and have fun!

Towards the end of the course, and during Project IS in Period 3 you will actually work with a Schnapsen game engine. Feel free to already play around with it.
